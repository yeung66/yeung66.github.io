2025 Annual Record

 2026.1.1  2026.2.19  Posts  

As per my annual tradition, I'm reviewing 2025 through a journal-style account and recording some thoughts.

Timeline

January

The BU and team started mass recruiting contractors to become the main force for project implementation going forward. Full-time employees needed to become so-called "architects" leading the contractors. Setting aside whether I, someone who had only been with the company for a year and a half, was qualified to be a so-called architect - in upper management's view, throwing more people at problems solves everything. Full-time employees too expensive? Then pile on contractors. Can the contractors handle it? Even if they can't, someone has to lead them. Even if efficiency is low, as long as the base number is large, it's still better than zero. So the boss started leading a contractor team, which gradually expanded to several times the size of the full-time employee count. Later, each full-time employee led several contractors, ultimately becoming a "labor contractor." For someone like me who had only been out of campus recruitment for two years, is being a labor contractor really a good thing?

Anyway, taking advantage of this contractor recruitment opportunity, I went to Changsha twice in January for communication and guidance with them. Changsha is a nice place. Although I didn't get to go anywhere fun during the business trip, the food was decent.

When I went home for Spring Festival, the family's new house was ready to move into. We moved to another residential complex with a better environment, bigger rooms, and easier parking spaces - very comfortable. Especially after the parking upgrade, I no longer need to ask my father for help parking the car when I drive back.

March

On the first day of March, while playing in Hong Kong, I suddenly saw news that Khalil Fong had passed away. Several people messaged me about it. As a long-time fan of Khalil Fong, my first reaction was bewilderment and disbelief - he had just made a comeback last year, released a new album, and appeared on shows, and suddenly I see such terrible news. Besides feeling sad for him, I increasingly felt that life is impermanent. Nothing truly lasts forever; people really can die suddenly, regardless of age or status.

At the end of the month, after performance reviews came out, I got my first good rating and promotion. However, looking at that bit of year-end bonus and salary increase, I felt it was just so-so. Even though this was called the best year, the money wasn't that much - especially comparing the year-end bonus between good and average performance ratings, the extra two months wasn't that much more money. I don't know what the point is (though maybe it's because my base salary isn't high enough). Also, I've always found it rather absurd that promotion is tied to performance. Promotion should mean your abilities have reached the next level, so promotion makes your level match your ability. However, performance only reflects the year's work - whether you met or exceeded expectations. The two aren't really related. I've even often seen lower-level employees assigned higher-level work and used as higher-level employees, but when it comes to performance reviews, because they're doing higher-level work, their targets and expectations are higher, resulting in not getting good performance ratings and naturally no promotion. It all depends on the boss's conscience.

April - June

During Labor Day holiday, my girlfriend and I went to Kunming. It was my first time back in Yunnan since graduating from elementary school. Kunming's weather is really comfortable, and Yunnan cuisine and mushrooms are delicious.

In June, the team outing was to Chongzuo, Guangxi again. Originally we considered keeping it relaxed and not too intense, but in the end it felt too relaxed - we didn't really go anywhere fun, and ultimately just played cards heavily with colleagues.

Going out to play was quite fun, although work was still there. Unfortunately, I still couldn't completely disconnect during holidays. Before, it was more about not daring to, afraid of being escalated. Now it's become more about worrying that things might go wrong or progress might stall, so I proactively check even during holidays.

August - November

The company planned to focus heavily on the Brazilian market, and the big boss suddenly thought about sending us tech people to Brazil for local support. Honestly, I don't understand what a tech person can accomplish by going to Brazil for a two-month business trip, but it was the boss's idea, and we could only comply. Initially, the manager collected everyone's preferences to see if anyone would volunteer. But obviously no one did - the time difference, security issues, language barrier, and long distance were all very discouraging factors. Eventually it became a rotation within the team - the manager went first, I was in the second batch, and even +2 couldn't avoid it.

After it was confirmed I was going, it was all kinds of preparations - applying for visa, booking flights, reserving hotels. During this process, I kept receiving all sorts of negative information about Brazil from social media and colleagues already stationed there - like poor security, poor infrastructure. The main concern was the security issue, with various thefts and robberies. I even saw a post during National Day about a Huawei employee being shot and their laptop stopping the bullet and saving their life. Really scary.

With this mindset, I departed for Brazil with another colleague after National Day, flying over twenty hours total before landing in SÃ£o Paulo. Then, being overly cautious - maybe I was dizzy from the flight - when I took an Uber from the airport to the hotel, I left my backpack in the car. It wasn't until over two hours later, after checking in and eating, that I realized I had left my backpack in the car - with my two laptops and an iPad inside. Although I immediately tried to contact the Uber driver, Uber's lost and found mechanism is absolutely ridiculous. You can't get the driver's contact information to reach them directly; you can only wait for the platform to forward your message to the driver, then have the driver reply to the platform to forward back to you. With all this back and forth, the driver didn't reply until the next morning. Plus, electronic products are really expensive locally in Brazil, so I never got it back. Filing a police report only got me a report receipt.

So my Brazil business trip started catastrophically - I lost all electronic devices except my phone right after landing, including my work computer and personal computer (two MacBook Pros). Losing the devices, my first reaction was actually heartache over the money, but my immediate thought was: my computer is gone, how am I supposed to work?? I had already accumulated two days of work matters during the flight, and now my computer was gone. Fortunately, I managed to get help from local IT and borrowed a computer. The tough part was that the loaner was only a Windows laptop - an old 2019 Dell notebook - but I had to make do. In the end, I got through it. Despite the time difference and crappy equipment, there were no major problems with work, I adapted well to life there, and I didn't encounter any security issues afterward.

Setting aside the accident and work, the Brazil business trip was actually quite interesting. Although security wasn't great, as long as you don't go out at night, only take taxis when going out, and don't look at your phone outside, you basically don't encounter major problems. In distant SÃ£o Paulo, I experienced quite a few new things and ate a lot of food - even ate quite authentic Chinese food. One Sichuan restaurant could even rival many restaurants back in China. Also, the two-month business trip incidentally earned me Marriott Platinum and Hilton Diamond status, and I started consciously building up hotel memberships.

Through this Brazil trip, I became even more aware that my English really isn't good enough, especially in speaking. Communicating with local colleagues was rather strained and difficult. I've been learning English all along, but I've never reached a level that satisfies me, which is really a pity.

December

After returning from Brazil, since all my devices were lost, I had to buy new ones. I was previously all-in on the Apple ecosystem and had been wanting to leave it, but if I bought another MacBook Pro + iPad, I'd be spending another 20k RMB, which I couldn't bear. So I switched to an Android tablet + Windows laptop and gradually adapted. I also gradually realized that these are just tools - basically anything can achieve the goal and meet requirements, it's just a matter of how smooth it feels. Of course, switching ecosystems was also because I wanted to experience something new.

Thoughts

Work & Life

As work becomes increasingly busy and high-pressure, work's proportion in life and its invasion into life has also increased. For example, in this year's annual review, most of it is actually work or work-related matters. There aren't many personal or life matters, and even the second half of the year was mainly due to the Brazil business trip. It's rather sad - work is gradually alienating me. The few days before New Year's, I actually took three days off to piece together an eight-day mini vacation, but I couldn't stop worrying about various projects. Even after turning off notifications, I still worried about work issues or if someone was looking for me, so I'd occasionally check in and reply to messages.

Work is becoming less and less interesting, not just because of the complaints mentioned above. During work, I increasingly feel the "cog in the machine" nature of technology. Business is the core; BD is way more important than tech. No matter how fancy the tech is, it's useless without business - no one will use it. For example, in the payment acquiring field, no matter how many features you provide, it's not as good as lowering the fees a bit more. Technology becomes "basic" at this point, and basic things naturally have difficulty arguing for value since everyone has them. Maybe all jobs at big companies are like this. Money & Investment

Work is boring; making money is the core. After opening a US stock account last year, riding the overall upward trend, I made a little profit in stocks. This year I also increased my principal investment and am accumulating and investing through different strategies across various accounts. However, in stock trading, I still haven't formed a trading system. Maybe I watched a few videos with analysis saying a certain stock is undervalued and bought in, then kept adding to the position as it went lower, and ended up deeply trapped. Investing is really a very professional matter that requires continuous time investment to learn. But under the current high-intensity work situation, the time and energy I invest in US stocks is actually decreasing. Maybe the ultimate destination is still the path of accumulating money through index funds.

Besides individual US stocks, this year I've been continuously adjusting my DCA approach for index ETFs. Initially, I just randomly DCA'd into NASDAQ and S&P 500 on Alipay. After opening the US stock account, I also bought some on-exchange ETFs there. But later I realized that for DCA into indices, domestic QDII funds would suffice, so there's no need to do it in the US stock account. Then in the second half of the year, NASDAQ suddenly became hot in the public eye again, and everyone started DCA into NASDAQ. Off-exchange funds started limiting quotas one after another - limits of fifty or a hundred, even some limiting to 10 yuan per day, which is ridiculous. Then some simply stopped accepting subscriptions, so these past two months I've shifted to on-exchange funds.

Looking back at the overall returns: Alipay funds made 9 percentage points, US stocks are still down 2 percentage points. Either way, both underperformed the broader market. Need to work harder and adjust more carefully next year.

During this process, I started regularly recording all assets this year - how much is in each account, how much in total. I set up a Google Sheet and record it once a month. Through this numerical recording method, I can very clearly see the changes in my savings. Saving money really isn't easy - maybe I'm just not earning enough. I'm still working toward saving my first million RMB, hoping to achieve it early this year.

The more I write, the more I feel like work has brought me too much negative energy, haha. The annual review and summary seems to have turned into a complaint session. Complaining is useless; I still need to do something to make a change. I've been reading The Compound Effect recently, which mentions that even doing small things, as long as you persist, will eventually be effective. In the new year, whether it's saving money, learning English, or life in general, I hope to do a little bit more every day.
